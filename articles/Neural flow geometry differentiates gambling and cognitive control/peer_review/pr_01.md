# Hard Peer Review — pr_01

**Manuscript:** `Neural flow geometry differentiates gambling and cognitive control despite matched sympathetic arousal` (`ds004511`, GG/CC/Rest)
**Reviewer role:** self-review (NoeticDiffusion research assistant), adversarial pass before external submission
**Overall recommendation:** Major revision before submission — the core finding is credible and well-audited, but the manuscript currently contains at least one factual mischaracterization of the primary task, one concrete data-accounting error, two internal methods inconsistencies, and several scope-of-claim problems that a competent external reviewer (especially one who checks the OpenNeuro README, which took five minutes) would flag immediately and would damage trust in every other number in the paper.

This is a stronger draft than most first-pass NDT clinical/task papers in this repository: the MNJ vs. MNPS dissociation is large, it survives a genuinely thorough seven-part robustness audit (intersection sample, epoch matching, LOOSO, bootstrap, permutation, artifact balance, five-confound partial correlation), and the EDA dissociation argument is a real scientific asset. But the manuscript oversells its confound coverage in places, and — most importantly — it describes the GG task incorrectly throughout, in the main text and in all four supplements. That single error, if caught by an external reviewer familiar with the dataset, would cost the paper a full review cycle on credibility grounds alone, independent of the statistics.

## Major findings

### 1. The Gambling Game (GG) task is described incorrectly everywhere it appears

This is the most serious problem in the manuscript.

The main text says (Methods, `sec:methods`):

> "(1) *gambling game* (GG) — a multi-round social decision task in which participants chose to cooperate or defect against a computerized partner, with monetary incentives"

The same "cooperate/defect against a computerized partner" framing also appears in `S1_Supplementary_methods.typ` ("spontaneous cooperate/defect decisions against a computerized partner with monetary incentive"), `S4_Supplementary_Clinical.typ` ("chose to cooperate or defect against a computer partner for real monetary stakes"), and `S5_Glossary.typ` ("chose to cooperate or defect against a computerized partner under real monetary stakes, involving spontaneous, uncertainty-laden, reward-driven decision-making").

I checked the primary source (`openneuro.org/datasets/ds004511`, mirrored at `github.com/OpenNeuroDatasets/ds004511`). The dataset is titled **"Deception_data"**, and the GG task is officially the **"Spontaneous Deception Task"**:

> "Participants were informed that the GG task aimed to study a player's behaviour during a gambling game. They were given SGD 50 at the start of the game. They were to undergo 144 rounds of making a prediction about the outcome of a dice roll. They were to also place a bet ranging from 10 cents to 80 cents for each prediction; they win the bet if the prediction was true and lose it if it was false. Participants were also informed that they were the only ones who knew the outcome of the dice roll and were responsible for reporting if their predictions were true to the system, and were debriefed at the end regarding this cover story."

There is **no computerized partner, no cooperate/defect choice, and no social decision element**. It is a solitary dice-prediction-and-self-report paradigm, specifically designed to give participants an unmonitored opportunity to lie about whether their prediction was correct (a classic spontaneous-deception elicitation design — the participant, not an opponent, is the one who may defect from the truth). The `src/ds004511_support.py` docstring already has this half-right ("GG = Gambling Game (spontaneous deception, 144 rounds)"), and `sciencelead/001.md` explicitly notes "The dataset name may be 'Deception_data,' and GG may involve spontaneous deception" — so the correct framing was known internally at some point but never made it into the manuscript text. The "cooperate/defect against a computerized partner" language does not appear anywhere in the pipeline config/manifest for this run either (I grepped `data/raw/neuralmanifolddynamics_ds004511_20260701_010029/` for "cooperate", "defect", "partner", "dice" — no hits), so this was not inherited from an upstream pipeline description; it was introduced during drafting and never checked against the source.

This matters beyond terminology. The Introduction's theoretical framing — "one context involving high-variance social prediction and risk" (`sec:intro`), and the Discussion's "gambling disrupts this with high-variance, outcome-dependent neural dynamics that generate strongly deformed, expansive flow fields" — builds a *social decision-making* and *outcome-driven reward* narrative on top of a task that is actually a self-report/deception paradigm with no social partner and no live outcome feedback loop of the kind implied. A reviewer who knows the dataset (or who simply reads the README) will read this as evidence that the authors did not carefully characterize their own primary task, which invites much harsher scrutiny of everything else.

**Required fix:** Rewrite every task description (main text §Methods/§Introduction/§Discussion, S1, S4, S5) to state accurately: GG is a solitary 144-round dice-prediction-and-betting task with a self-report/deception cover story (no partner, no cooperate/defect choice). Keep the existing, correct Limitations statement that trial-level outcome/deception labels are unavailable in the BIDS events — that caveat becomes even more important once the task is correctly described, because "spontaneous deception task" raises the obvious question of *whether the MNJ effect is partly a deception-related effect*, which the paper cannot currently address and must explicitly flag as an open question rather than avoid by mislabeling the task as a generic "gambling"/"social decision" paradigm.

### 2. The number of excluded GG sessions is wrong — three subjects have zero GG epochs, not one

The manuscript states (Methods, NMD pipeline):

> "Failed sessions: one GG session (sub-S210317) produced no valid epochs and was excluded from all analyses."

and (R1):

> "Subject S210317 (GG) was excluded from all analyses due to a failed EDF file. Forty-four to 45 subjects contributed data per task..."

I checked `results/ds004511_20260701/00_sanity_check/epoch_coverage.csv` directly. **Three** subjects have `gambling = 0`: `sub-S200203`, `sub-S200211`, and `sub-S210317` — not just `sub-S210317`. `sub-S200203` matches a documented issue in the official OpenNeuro README ("sub-S200203 does not have any EEG acquisition file pertaining to the Gambling Game task"). `sub-S200211` has no documented explanation in the README and needs to be traced back through the pipeline logs.

This also means the "44 to 45 subjects contributed data per task" statement in R1 is off for GG specifically: 45 total subjects − 3 with zero GG epochs = **42** with valid GG data (not 44), which is exactly why the intersection sample is N=42 — but the text implies GG dropped only one subject (45→44) when it actually dropped three (45→42). The underlying statistics (which use `.dropna()`/intersection logic) appear to have handled this correctly, so the *numerical results are very likely unaffected*, but the *narrative accounting of the sample* is factually wrong in two places, and this is exactly the kind of thing a statistical reviewer checks by cross-referencing a supplementary QC table against the text.

**Required fix:** Correct the exclusion count everywhere (Methods, R1, and the S1/S3 QC sections if they repeat the "one session" framing) to name all three subjects with zero GG epochs and give a reason for each (`sub-S200203`: documented missing GG acquisition per dataset README; `sub-S200211`: reason to be traced; `sub-S210317`: failed EDF file). Fix "44 to 45 subjects" → the correct per-task range (42 GG / 45 CC / 45 Rest, or whatever is confirmed after checking `sub-S200211`).

### 3. The main text misstates its own effect-size formula, contradicting S1 and the code

Main text, §Statistical design:

> "Effect sizes are reported as Cohen's $d$ (rank-biserial $r$ × $sqrt(N)$ approximation)."

I checked `src/ds004511_support.py::wilcoxon_paired`, which is the function actually used by every contrast script (`01_cross_task_mnps.py`, `03_mnj_reachability.py`, `05_mnj_confound_audit.py`):

```python
diff = a - b
stat, p = stats.wilcoxon(diff, zero_method="wilcox")
d = float(np.mean(diff) / (np.std(diff) + 1e-12))
```

This is the standard **paired mean-difference-over-SD ("Cohen's $d_z$")** formula, not a rank-biserial-$r$-to-$d$ conversion. `S1_Supplementary_methods.typ` already states the correct formula. The main manuscript text is simply wrong about its own method — likely inherited from a template/boilerplate sentence used in a different article that did use the rank-biserial approximation.

**Required fix:** Replace the Methods sentence with the accurate description: "Effect sizes are reported as Cohen's $d_z$, computed as the mean of the paired within-subject differences divided by their standard deviation."

### 4. The stated MNPS test count contradicts the paper's own results section

Main text, §Statistical design:

> "Multiple comparisons across the six MNJ metrics × three task pairs (18 tests for MNJ; 6 tests for MNPS)..."

But R2 correctly states "0 / 9 MNPS pairwise contrasts survived FDR 5%", and `results/.../01_cross_task_mnps/mnps_pairwise.csv` has 9 rows (3 MNPS axes × 3 task pairs = 9), matching S1's own "9 tests for MNPS pairwise." The Methods section's "6 tests for MNPS" directly contradicts the paper's own Results section three paragraphs later.

**Required fix:** Change "6 tests for MNPS" to "9 tests for MNPS" in §Statistical design.

### 5. The "not reducible to arousal" claim is validated only for GG vs. CC, but is rhetorically extended to the whole paper

The confound audit (R4, plus the dedicated Script 07) is real and thorough, but it tests **only the GG − CC difference** against five candidate confounds (HR, respiration rate, respiratory anchor index, EOG blink rate, tonic EDA). The abstract and conclusion, however, generalize this to the paper's overall claim: "ruling out peripheral sympathetic arousal as a confound" and "dissociated from peripheral sympathetic arousal (EDA tonic SCL GG = CC)" — stated without qualification, as if it covered all reported contrasts.

But the manuscript also reports and interprets substantial GG-vs-Rest effects (spectral radius $d = 1.94$, Frobenius $d = 0.73$, both FDR-surviving) and CC-vs-Rest effects (rotational power $d = -0.71$, MDR $d = 0.70$). Crucially, **tonic EDA does differ between GG and Rest** ($d = 0.53$, $p = 0.0006$, reported in R5 itself) and between CC and Rest ($d = 0.67$, $p < 0.0001$). So for the GG-vs-Rest and CC-vs-Rest contrasts, arousal has *not* been ruled out — it is a live, and on the EDA evidence probably partially correct, alternative explanation. The paper's strong, unqualified "arousal-independent" framing in the Abstract/Conclusion is only earned for the GG-vs-CC comparison.

**Required fix:** Scope the arousal-independence claim explicitly to GG vs. CC throughout (Abstract, Discussion, Conclusion). Add one sentence to R4 or R5 stating plainly that the GG/Rest and CC/Rest MNJ contrasts are *not* protected by the same confound audit and may in part reflect the arousal difference that is present for those pairs (Rest has markedly lower tonic EDA than both active tasks).

### 6. No conventional-EEG or raw-signal baseline is reported — the paper cannot currently rule out that MNJ is just tracking gross signal variance

GG and CC differ enormously in structure: GG is 144 rounds of self-paced dice-betting-and-reporting (852 mean epochs/subject, long sessions), CC is ~460 trials across four distinct RT/inhibition/conflict sub-tasks (~260 epochs/subject, much shorter sessions), and Rest is eyes-closed with no stimuli at all. This is about as large a task-structure contrast as is possible in EEG research. A hard reviewer's obvious question is:

> Would *any* reasonably sensitive EEG summary statistic — raw broadband signal variance, alpha/beta band power, or a simple Hjorth-complexity index — show a similarly large GG-vs-CC dissociation, without needing the Jacobian machinery at all?

The manuscript never reports this comparison. Given that the Frobenius norm of an estimated local Jacobian is, at some level, a function of how much the underlying signal moves around from moment to moment, a skeptical reviewer will suspect that at least part of $d = 1.58$ is recoverable from a one-line raw-power/variance contrast, and will ask for that control before accepting that MNJ is doing genuinely new work here (this is the same "added value over conventional EEG" objection that recurred in the psychosis and follow-up peer reviews in this repository, and it applies with even more force here because GG and CC are behaviorally so different).

**Required fix (or explicit scope-narrowing):** Either (a) add a compact baseline analysis showing that a simple raw-EEG summary (broadband variance or bandpower) does *not* reproduce the GG-vs-CC dissociation with comparable effect size, which would strengthen the paper considerably, or (b) explicitly state in Discussion/Limitations that the current design cannot rule out that part of the Frobenius/spectral-radius effect is attributable to more generic increases in signal variability during GG, and that incremental value over simple power metrics remains to be established.

### 7. Two available, untested channels could explain or moderate the effect and are not mentioned: event/stimulus rate and corrugator EMG

Two things in the dataset's own documentation are directly relevant to the GG > CC effect and are not used or discussed:

- **Event/response rate.** GG is a much longer, self-paced, low-event-rate task (bet placement + self-report per round, 144 rounds over a long session) versus CC's four back-to-back RT/inhibition/conflict sub-tasks (460 trials, much shorter session, presumably a much higher stimulus/response rate per unit time in at least some sub-blocks). Task-related evoked activity density is a mundane, non-"flow geometry" explanation for elevated raw EEG variability that the paper does not test or discuss.
- **Corrugator EMG.** The BioPac hardware recorded "two EMG channels" from the corrugator muscle (per the OpenNeuro README — a standard facial-affect / frowning-related channel, highly relevant to a gambling/self-report paradigm where wins and losses could produce differential facial affect). The manuscript mentions "two EMG channels" exist (§Dataset) but never uses them anywhere — not as a confound check (facial EMG can contaminate frontal EEG and inflate apparent "deformation"), and not as an exploratory affect-related analysis, despite being an unusually well-suited channel for this specific task.

**Required fix:** At minimum, add a sentence to Limitations naming these two unused signals as open confound/extension candidates. Better: run the two follow-up scripts proposed in the "Recommended follow-up work" section below.

### 8. "Outcome-dependent neural dynamics" contradicts the manuscript's own Limitations

Discussion (§`MNJ reveals task geometry invisible to global MNPS coordinates`):

> "...gambling disrupts this with high-variance, outcome-dependent neural dynamics that generate strongly deformed, expansive flow fields."

But Limitations states explicitly: "the `ds004511` behavioral records do not include trial-type labels in the BIDS `events.tsv` files: only synchronization pulses are recorded. We therefore cannot identify individual gambling trial outcomes (win/loss)..." The paper cannot simultaneously claim it has no outcome-level data and then attribute the neural effect to "outcome-dependent" dynamics in the same document. This is a small phrase but it is exactly the kind of internal contradiction a careful reviewer flags as evidence of rhetorical overreach beyond what the data show.

**Required fix:** Replace "outcome-dependent" with a term consistent with what was actually measured, e.g., "uncertainty- and decision-dependent neural dynamics" or simply drop the clause.

### 9. The strongest EAP-compatible result is right at the edge of its own correction threshold, and is given more rhetorical weight than that status supports

R6/Discussion repeatedly calls the CC `resp_anchor × m | HRV` result ($r = -0.41$, $p = 0.006$, "marginal FDR", $q \approx 0.051$) "the strongest single EAP-compatible finding" and "the strongest single EAP-compatible signal in this dataset." A $q$ of 0.051 against a pre-declared $\alpha = 0.05$ **fails** the paper's own correction criterion by a hair; it is presented as "marginal" rather than "non-significant after correction," which is defensible but borderline. Additionally, the "nine partial tests" family used to compute this FDR is not fully enumerated in the text — R6 separately reports uncorrected Rest correlations (`resp_anchor × rotational_power`, `resp_anchor × mdr`) that read as though they might or might not be part of the same family. A reviewer will ask exactly which tests constitute the correction family and whether it was pre-specified or assembled post hoc.

**Required fix:** State explicitly, in one place, the full enumerated list of tests that make up the nine-test EAP family (task × metric combinations), confirm it was specified before the tests were run, and tone down "strongest ... finding" language to something like "the numerically largest but FDR-non-significant EAP-compatible association." The manuscript already does most of this correctly in the "should be interpreted cautiously" paragraph — the fix is mainly to make the headline language in R6 as cautious as the paragraph that follows it.

### 10. Minor packaging/version items

- The dataset's own OpenNeuro README states **44** healthy individuals; the manuscript and EEGDash metadata both say **N = 45**. `epoch_coverage.csv` does show 45 subject folders in this pipeline run, so N = 45 is very likely correct for whichever dataset version was actually ingested — but the Data Availability statement cites `v1.0.0` while the README I checked is unversioned/latest (possibly `v1.0.2`). Worth a one-line footnote confirming subject count is version-specific and stating which version was actually processed.
- No file-path audit was performed as part of this review (unlike the psychosis hard review, which caught a missing cited CSV). Given the volume of newly-written S2/S3 tables, a final pass cross-checking every path cited in Data Availability and the supplements against files that actually exist on disk is cheap insurance before submission.

## Strengths

- The seven-part robustness audit (intersection sample, epoch-count matching, LOOSO, bootstrap, permutation, artifact balance, five-confound partial correlations) is genuinely thorough and is exactly the standard this repository should hold itself to.
- The MNPS-null / MNJ-strong dissociation is a clean, theoretically motivated, and now well-supported architectural claim for NDT — global coordinates missing what local flow geometry catches is a good story independent of the issues above.
- The EDA dissociation (R5) is a real scientific asset and, once its scope is correctly limited to GG vs. CC (see finding #5), remains one of the strongest single pieces of evidence in the paper.
- The manuscript is honest about the EAP results being exploratory and underpowered, and the Limitations section already correctly flags the missing trial-level GG labels — the infrastructure for appropriate caution exists; it just needs to be applied consistently (see #5, #8, #9).
- Figure and supplement coverage (S1–S5, six figures) is complete and internally well cross-referenced.

**All ten findings are now resolved.** No open action items remain from this review cycle other than the explicitly-flagged, permanently-open limitation that event/response-rate density cannot currently be reconstructed from the `ds004511` sync-pulse channel with confidence (Limitations).

## Bottom line

The defensible core of this paper is strong and does not need to change:

> A local-flow-geometry measure (MNJ) reveals a large, robustly audited task effect (gambling vs. cognitive control) that is invisible in global manifold coordinates and is not explained by tonic sympathetic arousal, at least for the gambling-vs-cognitive-control comparison specifically.

But the manuscript is not yet ready for external review. It currently contains a factual mischaracterization of its own primary task repeated across five files, an undercounted exclusion list that a QC-table cross-check catches in under a minute, two self-contradictory methods statements, an arousal-independence claim that overreaches beyond the one comparison it actually protects, and no baseline check against the most obvious "this might just be raw signal variance" objection. None of these are fatal to the core finding, and most are fixable without new data collection — but a reviewer who caught even two or three of them (the task mischaracterization alone would likely be caught by any reviewer with domain familiarity) would reasonably question the overall diligence of the manuscript and would very likely send it back for a full revision cycle rather than accepting with minor comments.
