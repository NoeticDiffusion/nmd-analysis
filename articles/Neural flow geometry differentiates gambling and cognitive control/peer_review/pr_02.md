My verdict:

> **Strong article candidate, but not submission-ready yet.**
> The primary MNJ result is now robust enough. The remaining blockers are mostly **metric-definition consistency, overclaim scoping, and one unresolved task-structure confound**.

# Overall assessment

This manuscript is now much stronger than the earlier ds003969 meditation branch. The core result is coherent:

> **Global MNPS coordinates do not separate GG/CC/Rest, but local MNJ flow geometry strongly separates especially GG from CC.**

The primary GG > CC MNJ Frobenius effect is large and unusually well audited: intersection sample, epoch-count matching, LOOSO, bootstrap, permutation, artifact checks, EDA, corrugator EMG, physiological confounds, and conventional EEG baselines all point in the same direction. The main article reports GG > CC Frobenius `d=1.62`, spectral radius `d=1.81`, robust bootstrap CIs, and EDA GG≈CC, which is the strongest part of the manuscript. 

The supporting supplements are also unusually strong. S3 shows the primary GG > CC Frobenius effect survives intersection sample, epoch-count matching, LOOSO, bootstrap, permutation, artifact balance, EDA, corrugator EMG, and conventional EEG comparison.  S2 shows the global MNPS null and the complete MNJ table, including 15/21 FDR-significant MNJ contrasts. 

So the primary result is **internally validated**.

But the paper still has three serious issues:

1. **Metric definitions are inconsistent across main text and glossary.**
2. **The “all three conditions” / rest interpretation is too broad in places.**
3. **The event/response-rate confound remains the main unresolved alternative explanation.**

---

# Recommendation

## Decision: major revision

Not because the result is weak — it is not. The revision is needed because the paper’s **mathematical contract must be airtight**. MNJ metrics are novel and reviewer trust will depend heavily on clear, consistent definitions.

I would not submit until the following are fixed:

1. Resolve all MNJ metric-definition inconsistencies.
2. Harmonize sample counts across main text, S3, S4, S5.
3. Downgrade broad claims about “all three conditions.”
4. Further soften the “cognitive/computational domain” interpretation.
5. Make the event/response-rate confound more prominent, or add a sensitivity analysis if possible.
6. Fix EAP wording so FDR-non-significant results are never made to sound positive.

# Major strengths

## 1. The primary MNJ result is genuinely strong

The central contrast is large, robust, and repeatedly cross-checked. The main text reports GG > CC Frobenius `d=1.62`, spectral radius `d=1.81`, and multiple robustness checks, including epoch-count matching, LOOSO, bootstrap, and permutation.  S3 independently documents intersection-sample `d=1.62`, epoch-matched `d=1.44`, LOOSO 44/44 sign consistency, bootstrap CI `[1.306, 2.128]`, and permutation `p<0.0002`. 

That is publishable-strength evidence for the **within-pipeline task-geometry claim**.

## 2. The MNPS-null / MNJ-positive dissociation is a good article spine

S2 reports no global MNPS contrast surviving correction across all nine pairwise tests, while MNJ contrasts are highly significant.  This gives the paper a clean conceptual contribution:

> The local flow layer contains task-context information not visible in global coordinates.

That is probably the best architectural validation of MNJ so far.

## 3. The EDA dissociation is important

The paper shows tonic EDA is elevated in both active tasks relative to rest but does not distinguish GG from CC, while MNJ Frobenius strongly does.  This is a very useful dissociation:

> GG vs CC is not simply “more arousal.”

The clinical supplement also translates this well and carefully limits the clinical meaning. 

## 4. The paper is unusually honest about limitations

The main article explicitly notes that trial-level deception/win/loss labels are absent, that event/response rate remains untested, that the dataset has an N=44 vs N=45 discrepancy, and that EAP results are exploratory/FDR-non-significant.  This is good scientific hygiene.

# Major concerns

## Major concern 1 — MNJ metric definitions are inconsistent

This is the most important fix.

The main text and glossary do not define several MNJ metrics the same way.

### Spectral radius

The glossary defines spectral radius as the largest-magnitude eigenvalue of the Jacobian. 
The main text describes it as the “largest singular value” / maximum local expansion rate. 

Those are not the same. The largest singular value is the operator norm. The spectral radius is the largest absolute eigenvalue.

**Required fix:** choose one and rename accordingly.

If the implementation uses eigenvalues:

> `spectral_radius = max_i |λ_i(J)|`

If the implementation uses singular values:

> rename to `operator_norm` or `largest_singular_value`.

Do not call a singular-value metric “spectral radius.”

### ACI

S5 defines ACI as the ratio of antisymmetric to symmetric component norms:

> `aci = ||J_anti||_F / (||J_sym||_F + ε)` 

The main text says ACI is a “ratio of compressive to expansive singular values” and later treats it as a structured/rotational measure. 

Those are different constructs.

**Required fix:** align the main text to the implemented formula. If ACI is anti/sym, then call it something like:

> “antisymmetric-to-symmetric deformation ratio”

Do not describe it as compression vs expansion unless that is actually the computation.

### MDR

The glossary defines MDR as diagonal magnitude divided by Frobenius norm. 
The main text describes it as “trace-normalized” or “net expansion vs contraction.” 

Those are not the same. Trace uses signed diagonal sum; diagonal magnitude / Frobenius uses unsigned axis-aligned deformation.

**Required fix:** define MDR as implemented, then interpret cautiously:

> “axis-aligned deformation fraction,” not “net expansion.”

### Why this matters

Reviewers will attack the MNJ layer if the metrics look renamed or unstable. Because the core result depends on these metrics, the paper must provide exact definitions in one table and use those definitions consistently everywhere.

This is a **submission blocker**.

## Major concern 2 — sample-count inconsistencies remain

The main text and S3 now use `N=44` for the intersection sample.  But S5 still says the intersection sample comprises `N=42 of 45 subjects`. 

That is a dangerous leftover. The article has already had a 44/45 discrepancy, so any additional mismatch will make reviewers suspicious.

**Required fix:** update every file to one consistent statement:

* total ingested subject directories: 45
* official participants table: 44
* valid GG subjects: 44
* valid CC: 45
* valid Rest: 45
* intersection sample: 44
* excluded from GG: sub-S200203

Then check S4, S5, figure captions, and data availability for the same numbers.

---

## Major concern 3 — “MNJ metrics strongly differentiated all three conditions” is slightly too broad

The abstract says MNJ metrics strongly differentiated all three conditions.  But S2 shows the strongest and cleanest separation is GG vs CC. Several CC vs Rest and GG vs Rest contrasts are metric-specific, and trace is entirely non-significant. 

A safer claim:

> “MNJ metrics strongly differentiated task context, especially the GG-vs-CC contrast, while rest comparisons showed metric-specific structure.”

Or:

> “The GG-vs-CC contrast was robust across all six non-trace MNJ metrics; GG-vs-Rest and CC-vs-Rest showed selective metric-specific differences.”

This protects the paper from overgeneralizing the task-space claim.

---

## Major concern 4 — the event/response-rate confound remains the biggest unresolved alternative

The paper is honest about this, but I would make it even more central. The limitation states that GG is long and self-paced, while CC is a fast, back-to-back task battery, and event/response density remains untested. 

This is not a minor limitation. It is the main remaining non-geometric alternative:

> GG may differ from CC because the temporal structure of task events and responses differs dramatically.

The conventional EEG baseline helps, but it does not close this. Corrugator EMG helps, but it does not close this. EDA helps, but it does not close this.

**Best fix, if feasible:** add one crude event-density sensitivity analysis. Even without trial labels, possible options are:

* use available sync/button pulses as event-density proxies,
* split GG and CC into time-matched early/middle/late blocks,
* compare only low-event-density CC windows if identifiable,
* include epoch-level local variance of button/sync proximity as a covariate,
* compute MNJ on temporally downsampled CC to match GG’s approximate pacing.

If not feasible, move the limitation higher and say:

> “The current study establishes a robust task-context MNJ difference, not a process-pure gambling-specific mechanism.”

This is probably the most important interpretive boundary.

---

## Major concern 5 — the EAP result is still too positive in the Author Summary

The main text is mostly disciplined: EAP is secondary, exploratory, and FDR-non-significant. But the Author Summary says respiratory breathing patterns are associated with reduced flow deformation during cognitive control. 

That is too strong, because the cognitive-control result is `resp_anchor × m`, not flow deformation/Frobenius, and it is FDR-non-significant. The GG flow-deformation trend is sign-stable but non-significant.

Suggested replacement:

> “Secondary exploratory analyses suggested possible associations between respiratory anchoring and neural geometry, but these did not survive correction and require replication.”

This prevents category drift.

# Medium concerns

## 1. “Arousal-independent” should remain scoped to GG vs CC only

The abstract already scopes the EDA dissociation to GG vs CC and notes that EDA differs from rest.  Keep that boundary everywhere, including title-adjacent language and clinical summary.

Good phrase:

> “matched sympathetic arousal for the GG-vs-CC contrast.”

Avoid:

> “arousal-independent neural geometry” as a global statement.

## 2. The clinical supplement is good, but should not imply future clinical value too strongly

S4 is careful and repeatedly says no clinical intervention, no individual prediction, no biomarker, no patient population.  That is good. I would keep it, but make the clinical supplement optional rather than prominent for the first submission unless targeting a clinically oriented venue.

## 3. Conventional EEG baseline helps, but does not prove incremental validity

The paper says the largest conventional EEG GG-vs-CC effect is relative theta power `d=0.63`, less than half the MNJ Frobenius effect.  That is useful, but effect-size comparison is not the same as incremental predictive validity.

A stronger future analysis would be:

* model 1: conventional EEG features
* model 2: conventional EEG + MNJ
* cross-validated task classification or mixed-effects model
* show added variance / AUC / likelihood improvement

For now, phrase as:

> “MNJ effects are larger than tested conventional summaries,” not “MNJ adds independent predictive information.”

## 4. Rest comparisons are structurally different because rest is eyes-closed

The paper notes rest is eyes-closed and blink rate confirms that. Good. But this means GG/CC vs Rest contrasts partly reflect eyes-open active tasks vs eyes-closed rest. The GG-vs-CC contrast is the clean contrast. Rest should be used as a reference state, not a symmetrical third task.

# Revised claim ledger

## Internal validated

1. Global MNPS `m/d/e` does not robustly separate GG, CC, and Rest at session level. 
2. GG vs CC MNJ Frobenius and spectral radius effects are very large and robust. 
3. The GG > CC Frobenius effect survives intersection sample, epoch-count matching, LOOSO, bootstrap, and permutation. 
4. Tonic EDA does not differ between GG and CC and does not covary with the Frobenius difference. 
5. Corrugator EMG does not explain the GG-vs-CC effect. 
6. Tested conventional EEG summary effects are smaller than the MNJ Frobenius effect. 

## Plausible interpretation

1. GG involves higher local neural flow-field deformation than CC.
2. CC/rest have relatively more rotationally structured flow than GG.
3. MNJ is more sensitive than global MNPS for this task-context contrast.
4. The GG-vs-CC MNJ effect is not a simple peripheral arousal effect.

## Exploratory

1. Respiratory anchoring may relate to lower mobility during CC.
2. Respiratory anchoring may weakly moderate Frobenius during GG.
3. Rest-condition respiratory-anchor associations with rotational power/MDR.

## Not supported

1. Deception-specific neural geometry.
2. Trial-level win/loss or lying effects.
3. Causal EAP.
4. Clinical biomarker claims.
5. Full arousal-independence for rest contrasts.
6. Exhaustive proof that MNJ is independent of all conventional EEG representations.

# Final hard verdict

This is the strongest article branch you currently have in the NMD task-geometry line.

The primary claim is defensible:

> **MNJ flow geometry differentiates gambling and cognitive control despite matched tonic sympathetic arousal, while global MNPS coordinates do not.**

But the article is only as strong as the precision of the MNJ measurement contract. Right now, the data and robustness are strong; the remaining weakness is **definition discipline**.

Fix the metric definitions and sample-count inconsistencies, keep EAP explicitly exploratory, and either address or foreground the event/response-rate confound. Then this becomes a solid, submit-worthy methods-anchored empirical paper.
